    Kerika


    Latest Update - May 28, 2007 - KERIKA 1.1 IS RELEASED!

    We are pleased to announce the release of Kerika version 1.1, a JXTA-based Java application that provides a visual context for team collaboration, with support for process templates, document management and sharing of large files. We released a beta version a year ago, that some of you may have tried out, and based upon the feedback we got we have made a bunch of improvements:

    1. Sharing projects just became a whole lot easier: previously: just add email address to the project's team list and Kerika takes care of the rest. Other Kerika users who are online get a full set of project information (Idea Pages, content, etc.), while a storage server holds messages for offline users. But best of all: if your team includes people who are not Kerika users, they get their project updates sent to them automatically as emails. This means that you don't have to get everyone in the team to use Kerika before you start using it!

    2. We now have more tightly synchronized views for all members of a project: whenever someone moves an item on an Idea Page, or extends a project, or makes some visual changes, or adds content, or attaches notes to items, or deletes items, or... these changes get reflected for all members of the team. Which means that everyone on the team always has the big picture, which we call "sharing content in context". Every Idea Page has its own Trash (Recycle Bin), so if any member of the team deletes an item from a project, any other team member can restore it later.

    3. The code is all in Java, so go ahead and try it out on Macs, Windows and Linux. The UI looks identical on all platforms because we built our widget set, which is based upon the Mac's Aqua style.

    4. For added privacy, users can set up their own "private storage server" on any machine they have so long as it is reliably accessible by the rest of the team. By setting up a private server you can create a "ring of trust": if any of your team members are offline, updates intended for them will be stored on your private server, which operates entirely under your control.

    The popular document management feature works as before: you can attach notes to documents, and whenever anyone edits a shared document, this modified version is sent to everyone else on the team; Kerika takes care of all the filing for everyone's computers.

    Please take a look at one of the Flash demos we have available at http://www.kerika.com/flash_demos.html and then give this JXTA app a whirl! Getting started is pretty simple: signup by providing a name and email address at https://sales.kerika.com/myaccount.php; then click on the link in the confirmation email we send you to get your personal License Key. After downloading and installing Kerika, start it up and give it your License Key and you are good to go.

    Your feedback would be much appreciated.

    Dextrus Prosoft Consulting is a Technology Project Management Consulting and Systems Development Services company. Their project management methods are based on best project management practices learned through the varied consulting practices and engagements. Their method allows them to assist their clients by:

    • mitigating project's risks,
    • clearing communications,
    • maintaining and managing healthy project environment and
    • adopting a successful project management model applied to customer projects.

    Their systems development methods address the business needs, make a fine distinction between the technological and corporate goals and fulfills the strategic objectives of deploying technology targets.

    Their current product is "SENTINEL" - an ideal solution for managing and monitoring the connected enterprise network. SENTINEL is a lightweight tool that is distributed across the network, and utilizes lightweight modules residing in numerous locations around the network. These modules are fully aware of the environment around them and they are intelligent enough to make an informed decision about the problems occurring in their vicinity. They are also aware of other peer monitoring modules around them. And they actively share their knowledge with them.

    The P2P paradigm fits neatly into the Sentinel proposed solution. Distributed lightweight peers cooperating to solve a complex problem, which was the dream sold by P2P frameworks. It was a lofty goal till the emergence of commonly accepted protocols under the JXTA P2P framework. Most of the other P2P technologies are designed for file transfers, and Dextrus' plans required more than just P2P file transfers. JXTA has made P2P a viable alternative to the client-server architecture, providing the most flexible, generic and standards based P2P platform available currently.

    With JXTA it has become very easy to rapidly implement a P2P system, which is platform independent and easily available. As such Dextrus Prosoft plan to use JXTA to implement their solution. And with the emergence and gradual acceptance of the JMX (Java Management Extensions) framework by the monitoring industry it would be possible in the near future to write management tools which work with each other. They plan to use the JMX framework extensively along with J2SDK and J2EE to designing a scalable monitoring solution for large networks. JXTA allows them to distribute the processing load among a number of monitoring nodes and thereby eliminating the need for bigger and expensive centralized server. (Compare this to the traditional way to solve the scalability problem in monitoring solutions is to deploy on bigger, faster hardware!)

    Looking back at the decision to choose JXTA Technology, Dextrus Prosoft feel that JXTA is the right way to go with respect to scalable P2P solutions. In the future they see JXTA becoming an integral part of the computing solutions which need to be scalable and which need to work on diverse platforms and environments - JXTA can become glue between complex distributed applications.

    Dextrus invites the JXTA Community to participate by joining their JXTA Community project "SENTINEL".

    NTT Network Innovation Laboratories


    At NTT Network Innovation Laboratories, researchers are studying intelligent networks. They hope to support the evolution of various network communities which will reflect human society in the real world. They have been involved investigating the use of JXTA technology since Summer 2002 when the Japanese mailing list ja-jxta was launched.

    One of the researchers, Koichi Takasugi, has developed a seamless service platform using JXTA technology. This platform, called Seamless Stream Browsing, provides continuous content-delivery services even if the mobile content-delivery terminal is disconnected from the network, or user switches the content-browsing terminals. He is working on this project with Motonori Nakaura, from NTT's Circumstances Adaptive Online Storage System (CAOSS) group.

    Since this platform is based on JXTA technology, it immediately discovers a substitute or more adequate content-delivery terminal. It is a serverless and completely-distributed system designed to support dynamic network environments. NTT is using the Java implementation of JXTA technology. This product is intended for use by NTT customers.

    Takasugi found JXTA to be the perfect technology for his project for multiple reasons:

    • It is distributed as open source.
    • There is a wide range of JXTA resources available both online and hardcopy.
    • Online support is available on the JXTA mailing lists. (Developers of the JXTA platform were especially helpful!)
    • Platform independence is required for our platform.

    Once development began, the team found significant development cost and time savings. They could focus on their application's functionality, relying on JXTA protocol to seamlessly manage device interactions. Takasugi believes that the big attraction of P2P is its inherent flexibility. Applications using JXTA for P2P are not restricted by network topology and communication scaling. Anonymous communication between devices is an equally important point for pervasive computing environments. They hope that as more people are drawn to JXTA technology, the reliability and performance are developed even further than what is available today.

    Koichi Takasugi works with the developers of JXCube (JXTA eXtreme Cube) project, especially regarding continuity and service mobility issues.

    SparkNet S.A.

           

    SparkNet S.A. is an ISP operating in Greece since 1995. It is proud to have been the first to achieve several major accomplishments. It is the first to:

    • be licensed by the Greek National Telecommunications and Post Commission,
    • offer ADSL connections in Greece,
    • offer Internet Roaming through an exclusive agreement with GRIC.

    Their project “Provision and synthesis of services over a P2P network” began March 2003. It is co-financed by the Greek Secretariat for Research and Technology. The aim of this project is to investigate and propose a P2P application where peers could sell services either alone or as a bundle combining their own offers with offers made by other peers. In this setting, a peer could be any small or medium enterprise which has a service to sell. The idea of dynamically creating business alliances and offering bundles by synthesizing the services of many small enterprises could lead to the provision of complete solutions which otherwise only large corporations could supply.

    JXTA technology provides the platform on top of which SparkNet can build a “proof-of-concept” application where different tourist operators would be able to combine and sell services. Operators can form their own groups (using the underlying JXTA notion of a group) representing dynamic alliances, advertise and search for services (using the JXTA resolver protocol) and charge for services (using an add-on protocol being developed).

    From the start, it was maintained that any solution will have to be independent of current practices. This was to ensure that any solution implemented will also be supported three to four years from now. By that time, current assumptions made about the operating environment may be invalid. Initially, the SparkNet development team looked into Gnutella as an alternative. They also examined different architectures like Morpheus, Napster and HyperCup. However, none of these technologies provided the flexibility required. JXTA was the only technology that was flexible and independent of underlying architectures. It was a critical decision when defining the product path.

    This flexibility and independence were strong benefits that they gained from using JXTA technology. An additional benefit, they also have access to a specification document, access to a reference implementation and access to a large set of resources. Furthermore, JXTA ties well with Java, the chosen implementation platform. They also feel that the backing of a strong company like SUN provides assurances about the future of the technology. These are all benefits that are important for a success of a project. A final benefit comes from the use of XML for the description of services. They believe that the move towards a semantic description of services will be vital for the adoption of P2P systems.

    SparkNet S.A. is a strong proponent of open solutions not tied to specific platforms. Thus, the company utilizes the whole suite of JAVA technologies (J2EE, Java Web Services and Jini). It has implemented and employed a number of Java-based products including portals, e-learning systems, etc.

    When asked about SparkNet's vision for JXTA technology, Nicholaos Petalidis, the Research and Development Manager says "We believe that as broadband becomes available to the end customer, focus will move to the edges of the network at the points where information is created. P2P software will be in a unique position to take advantage of this situation. We envisage therefore various virtual P2P networks where people dynamically select repackage and offer information and services. We hope that JXTA will mature into a globally accepted standard for setting up such networks."

    The SparkNet team has recently started participating in the JXTA community discussions. They plan to participate in the recently proposed JXTA Community project Nexus. They also plan to participate in project Venezia-Gondola which fits in their overall vision of the kind of P2P services that will be required in the near future.

    Peercom

    Peercom, located in Seoul, Republic of Korea (South Korea), was established in August 2003 under the catch phrase of "human-centered technology and the system development." They work towards this goal by developing and providing middleware software for ubiquitous computing. Their software creates building blocks for new technologies based upon loosely-coupled distributed computing technologies such as P2P, messaging, etc.

    The main product of the Peercom, called "My Personal Portal", is the service that shifts today's personal computing environment from device-oriented to a human-centric environment. Also called the "personal digital asset management solution," it manages an individual's invaluable digital contents by integrating all devices. It also enables users to approach and utilize any digital content such as (PIMs, mail, files, photos, movies, addresses, my favorites, etc) at anytime and anywhere. My Personal Portal will distribute the most recent version of these digital files, without any physical restriction of time, or spaces like devices or network environment.

    "My Personal Portal" consists of three main services:

    1. My DeskStation - a window program, which provides replicating and managing peers' contents sorted by data types,
    2. My WebSpace - a web service, which provides a personal web content storage (i.e Internet Disk, Web-hard)
    3. Shared WebSpace - a community web service, members can create their own groups and share their contents by shared webspace (Internet group storage )

    Benefits of these services are:

    • Can access personal remote PCs and use data with IE (Internet Explorer) or Peercom's own desktop program.
    • Can replicate personal data (Files, Folder, MP3, Photos, Images, E-mail, Address, Bookmark..) between PCs and work with latest data at any PC
    • Can store personal data at Internet storage (webspace) and share that data with others.
    • Can make any PC as a personal homepage server.

    Members can get to their WebSpace and Shared WebSpace from a web browser and also directly from "My DeskStation." Features of the Peercom service are:

    • Easy to install / use without complicate network setting.
    • Connect an individual's PCs, PDA, Mobile phones, etc.
    • Share digital data with any digital devices with no waiting period.
    • Home PC can be used as a homepage web server.
    • DCRL( Digital Content Resource Locator) can address any PC's resources such file, movie, photo, mp3, etc
    • Custom-built service

    "My Personal Portal" uses JXTA as a communication protocol between devices such as PCs and mobile phone. During initial design of their product, Peercom reviewed other P2P technologies such as Groove Networks or Gnutella. They soon found many limitations to use them as a standard platform for various P2P applications. These issues focused their development team on using JXTA Technology, which shortened the required product development time (although there were some bugs found).

    A key factor in the decision is the network independance which makes the communication between peers possible in any network environment. JXTA Technology gives more and more clear meanings in networking where presently most PCs use Firewall, NAT, or Dynamic IP. JXTA Technology also improves development productivity for network-enabled applications. This supports many types of devices, and the implementation in other device environment such as PDA, cell phones, etc. has gotten much simpler. Selecting JXTA Technology shortened the time needed for developing their new product.

    Other technologies used to develop "My Personal Portal" are XML-based Personal Repository, J2SE, Combride, JXTA-based P2P, Peercom's own P2P Protocol, etc. Other Sun technologies currently used include JAVA, JNI to develop a portal for web interfaces.

    The version 0.9 alpha release of "My Personal Portal" will be released on 8 March, 2004. A short English-language demo service will be available mid-March 2004.

    Peercom is also working on a mobile P2P project based upon JXTA Technologies. This work is being done for SK Telecom, which is currently one of the largest mobile telecommunication company in South Korea. The pilot of Mobile P2P is currently underway.

    Peercom developers believe that P2P best applies to embedded devices rather than on general PCs. They expect that JXTA will be the choice for the platform of ubiquitous computing in the future. By porting JXTA-C with Phone OS as a starter, they strive to become the expert company in the field of ubiquitous middleware platform technology by applying JXTA in various embedded devices.

    Other JXTA Activities: Peercom has been hosting the Korean JXTA forum for 2 years now. They are the only Korean company publically involved in JXTA research. The Peercom development team also participates in "Tcp Transport" and "Rendezvous Service" tasks by participating in the jxta-c "Borabora" project (Jxta-C 2.x Implementation).

    Nokia

    The Automated Network Services (ANS) at Nokia is located in Scotts Valley & Mountain View, CA. They're active on JXTA discussion lists and have participated in JXTA Town Hall meetings.

    ANS team members are pictured left to right: Felipe Albertao, Gretel Baumgartner, Nipa Shah, Anson Lee.

    Nokia is in the mobile phone business, but that's not all! This department specializes in Secure Content Management, specifically Nokia's anti-virus and anti-spam product Nokia Message Protector. The Automated Network Services (ANS) is a web-based server that provides software updates to network appliances, including Nokia Message Protector.

    ANS is a distributed server, installed at different sites around the world. ANS uses JXTA to communicate between the individually installed machines. In effect, every ANS machine is a peer in a peergroup. It is easy to add an additional server; using JXTA advertisements and discovery service, the new server will locate and join the peergroup. ANS uses JXTA sockets to communicate data events within the system. ANS performs its database replication and synchronization using the JXTA socket.

    During the requirements definition phase of ANS, several requirements made JXTA the obvious choice. The ability to provide flexible network topologies for the installation of ANS at different sites. Using JXTA, they can configure the ports and protocol used to get through existing firewalls so that sites can communicate. Using JXTA, nodes can be easily added and removed. JXTA provides the network communication layer. And, since they are already Java programmers, the learning curve was that much shorter!

    The team's vision for JXTA and P2P is Web services with 100% up-time, affordable, any time, anywhere. They believe that P2P will commoditize the availability and scalability aspects of online operations, by delivering a platform where applications are truly decentralized and ubiquitous. Instead of spending a huge amount of resources up-front, P2P will allow businesses to grow its IT operations on demand, yet still providing a secure and high-available infrastructure.

    Tryllian

    Tryllian, based in Amsterdam, Netherlands, provides tools for agent-based distributed computing. The tools are used by system integrators and corporations for integration of business processes, supply chain, web and wireless, and in other areas requiring complex business integration.

    Agents are autonomous and adaptive software components, providing rich, flexible services. A multi-agent system architecture allows you to break up complexity into relatively simple agents, and therefore, relatively simple relationships between the agents. Because the agents are adaptive, location-independent and loosely coupled, Tryllian's agent-based systems are extremely flexible and easy to upgrade and change. This agent-based architecture, together with Tryllian's tools enable rapid and low-impact integration of distributed, multi-platform, heterogeneous systems that work across organizations and along the supply chain.

    Tryllian uses JXTA technology for communication between agents (i.e., between components of a system), next to JMS and Web Services. JXTA protocols are used for the transport of agents across a dynamic network. JXTA protocols are also the key for agents to find their way in a network. On top of JXTA technology, Tryllian provides a custom-built agent security infrastructure based on TLS.

    Tryllian uses standardized components and technologies where possible. Using JXTA and Java technologies has allowed Tryllian to concentrate on its core business: building tools and infrastructure for distributed multi-agent systems. Tryllian sees decentralized architectures as key in achieving the next-level of computing interoperability.

    Clay International

    Clay International is a small IT consulting firm that designs and develops software solutions for clients in various industries such as banking, utilities, and airlines. They developed Kappie as a way to learn some new technologies and have some fun.

    Kappie is an information manager that lets you create, download, organize and share virtually any kind of digital data. It works by passing items from person to person. When you share an item, you can see how many people have received it and how they have rated it. Kappie is a unique product that combines elements from some of today's most popular applications: email, file sharing, instant messaging, weblogs, and social networks.

    Kappie uses the JXTA-C implementation as the foundation for its transport layer, as well as to manage user presence. Protocols used include discovery, rendezvous, and pipe binding. They chose JXTA mostly because of the fact that it is open source, which ensures a rigorous level of peer review and allows for customization. Since Kappie is written in C++, it was a requirement that their P2P library have a C/C++ interface. Most importantly, since JXTA is non-proprietary and is maintained by a large community of developers rather than a single company, Clay developers believe it has great potential to become a standard technology on which many interoperable applications will be built.

    Prior to undertaking this development, they considered Jabber, Joltid, Gnutella, and Groove, but none of these alternatives had all the benefits of JXTA described above. Additionally, Kappie was developed on a very tight budget, so the most practical benefit was that it was free! Other than that, having a peer group of knowledgeable developers to interact with and draw knowledge from was hugely valuable to the development team.

    Vision for P2P and JXTA

    Many people are excited about P2P because it has the potential to fulfill the long-promised notion of allowing anyone to become an information producer, rather than just a consumer. To date, this worthy goal has been hampered by several problems: market fragmentation and incompatible technologies; simplistic applications that only support one mode of communication; and legal issues over copyright protection. JXTA, with its open protocols and rich set of services, offers solutions to the first two problems.

    Kappie is one of the first applications to demonstrate that P2P is about more than chatting and sharing bootleg music. The power of many-to-many computing should be leveraged to help people find each other, share their thoughts and expertise, solve problems more quickly, and express themselves creatively. JXTA enables all of these things, but no "killer application" has yet emerged to take advantage of its massive scalability. They hope that Kappie and other applications targeted at the mass market will help propel JXTA to the front of the P2P pack.

    Jonathan Clay been an active developer in the JXTA-C project for over a year.

    Morgan Spenser Consulting

The S2S Provider Peers maintain a local index of the fulltext and metadata of all scientific documents found. Via S2S Hub Peers, the super-peers of the network, search and download requests of users will reach the provider. Users will search for, browse and download information using S2S Consumer Peers.

    Science-To-Science implements the JXTA Search specification and extends various conceptual as well as technical aspects:

    • As the S2S Provider Peers automatically scan various local datasources for new or updated documents researchers will not need to adapt their normal workflow of editing and publishing scientific information. Combining this with locally managed fulltext indexes a much higher up-to-dateness and completeness can be achieved than is possible with traditional centralized approaches.
    • S2S Hub Peers also aggregate search results sent by the S2S Provider Peers and automatically remove duplicate hits. Aggregated search results are cached and completed with late-arriving results to enhance and speed up subsequent searches.

    Center for Realtor Technology

    The Center for REALTOR® Technology (CRT) is a department within the NATIONAL ASSOCIATION OF REALTORS® (NAR) that focuses on providing a voice for REALTORS® with the technology vendors that service them. CRT's mission is to be forward thinking in understanding and assessing the impact of technology on their REALTOR® Membership.

    As the use of the Internet as an information source has grown, the posting of property listing information has become commonplace. Many Web sites that maintain property data allow users to search their listing database for properties of interest. However, listing data is widely distributed; it is (appropriately) in the hands of agents, brokers, MLS's, CIEs, etc. The ability to perform a search for a property requires the site administrator to aggregate the needed data. This type of solution introduces the significant complexity associated with maintaining data retrieved from disparate systems.

    CRT's Ernestine P2P project was born of a desire to eliminate these complexities. The Ernestine solution uses a peer-to-peer (P2P) approach to the problem of providing property listing search capabilities on the Web. The P2P paradigm allows distributed data sources (databases, XML, spreadsheets, etc.) to participate in a search without the need for the upload of data to a central repository. By allowing owners to maintain their data, updates are reflected immediately making the most current information always available. In addition, information returned as a result of a search is easily linked back to the Web site that represents the data source from which the information was obtained.

    The JXTA framework was chosen because of its open source nature and leadership position in this space. It provides most of the basic functionality necessary for the Ernestine P2P system. Its protocol agnostic design and XML messaging is a natural fit for this solution. This has resulted in reduced development time and allows the solution to be widely distributed at a lower cost.

    Going forward, CRT will continue to investigate the use of this technology to connect widely distributed data sources. P2P technologies have a great potential to lower the cost of doing business for REALTOR® vendors' and in turn lower costs for NAR members. The potential to search several sources of listing data at once, versus site-by-site searches, will be a great efficiency boost for any user of this system. Using P2P technologies, such as JXTA, will allow CRT to contribute significantly to converting this promise to reality.

    The CRT has found JXTA to be a powerful framework that fits in with the vision they have concerning the direction of this technology. They have had great success creating an architecture around it that they believe will make their Ernestine P2P system a success. CRT has gained a great deal of experience that will be shared with the JXTA community in the coming weeks in the form of lessons learned and code releases based on their Ernestine project.
