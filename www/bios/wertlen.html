Developer Spotlight


Ron Wertlen, also known as rw on jxta.org discussion threads, is married and lives in Berlin, Germany. He works for neofonie GmbH, which was a previous JXTA Company Spotlight. Ron happily updates the JXTA Community that neofonie's Science-To-Science project described in that spotlight is now being released to public science research institutions!

Ron has been working on Project JXTA since first quarter of 2002. He follows the discussion threads every day. He participates by helping out wherever he can, occasionally submitting a bug patch or a detailed bug report, and other times supporting other users. He hopes to get more involved in developing Project JXTA. Walkers and autonomic topologies leave a lot of room for new ideas and implementations, especially the work going on in the platform regarding SRDI. He also sees developments in JXTA security quite fundamental to establishing JXTA as a serious production weight network and is quite excited about Jambalaya for this reason.

He reads the mailing lists every day because besides notification of fixes, events etc., he finds there are a lot of good ideas out there, especially technical ideas. He also likes to participate virtually in JXTA meetings and gives his opinion where it is constructive. He promotes JXTA daily through his work at neofonie. He always participates in JXTA Community votes, lending his support to the JXTA Community in the simplest and yet most basic way.

Ron believes that there are several benefits to using JXTA. First of all, JXTA leads the standardization effort in an area where closed source, proprietary protocols are the rule. Thus developing a network application which speaks JXTA is a sure way to maintain interoperability with other services. This allows usage scenarios which are related to Grid enterprises, but which have a stronger autonomic and decentralized flavor. With Version 2 of JXTA, developers also get a powerful Java framework for building distributed decentralized social (P2P) applications. He continues saying that getting the most out of the JXTA platform does require some knowledge of the complex structures and processes in the platform, but there is always documentation or someone online to help out. Using JXTA is easy and it's the best way to learn about the technology.

He believes that custom applications which speak JXTA but use proprietary technologies will emerge. These will take advantage of application specific optimizations, but will also take part in the global network. A powerful open source development kit based on current efforts (Ezel, JXTA Sockets, etc.) will continue to allow general applications to emerge. In a way, the development of Project JXTA and the social interactions in its community and other communities based on open principles, show the way to a possible future world: a world in which communication is open and is used to bring people together, to foster understanding and to build a fair and peaceful society. JXTA shows that all peers can be equal (from a protocol standpoint) despite their unique capabilities.

Beyond computers, he is keenly interested in current developments in microbiology and genetics. Specifically symbiosis and immunology fascinate him. The balance between destruction and creation, regulation and freedom, in the networks within us is astounding. This science also goes to show what people so often forget, that their vehicle through life - this body we each possess - is a marvel.
