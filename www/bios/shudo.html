Developer Spotlight


Kazuyuki Shudo is known as shudo on the JXTA mailing lists. He is located in Tsukuba, Japan and has been working on Project JXTA since Spring of 2002. He is a developer of a JXTA-based middleware project called Personal Power Plant (P3). He works on JXTA Technologies about 10 hours a week. He is working to improve the reference implementation (RI) of JXTA through development and use of the JXTA-based middleware.

When first researching P2P technologies (Spring 2002), Shudo and the GTRC/AIST team team looked into traditional technologies like Gnutella which offered flooding. They, then, turned their attention to modern and smart technologies like Tapestry and Pastry with DHT. In Autumn 2002, they chose JXTA as the basis of their development. The reasons they selected Project JXTA included:

  • its large user base,
  • the stability of the API,
  • the NAT traversal feature,
  • the decentralized architecture and
  • inherent scalability.

The team wanted to provide the NAT traversal feature but did not want to develop their own from scratch. The team also felt that the scalability of Project JXTA was an important feature for P3. Looking forward, they also believed that, by basing their project on JXTA, they paved the road for easy integration of new engineers, since any engineer with experience in JXTA Technology would be able to smoothly start working on the project. GTRC expects JXTA Technologies to continue evolving since it has such incredible momentum driven by the large user base. Because P3 is based on the JXTA API standard, it will remain compatible with future versions, even as both P3 and JXTA Technology grows and evolves.

Shudo found he could utilize JXTA's P2P abilities like identification, discovery and grouping of peers and NAT traversal, without development of our own similar P2P infrastructure. This means that JXTA could be applied to P3 even though one of its goals is high-performance computing and different from usual P2P software. To his company, this is a big advantage because they could hire engineers who have an experience on using JXTA and they could smoothly start working on their project. This is an advantage of using JXTA's established API.

When asked about his vision for JXTA technology he says "Vast number of devices, software and computers support our life more widely and essentially. JXTA may be the technology which enables such a society and otherwise it has made an impact on the vision." In the near future he hopes that JXTA technology will become more scalable, high-performance and light-weight. They are important for the purposes of P3.

He is the developer of a Java JIT compiler shuJIT. The JIT is for x86 processors / Linux, FreeBSD and NetBSD. It has been distributed and widely used since 1998.There have been been over 12,000 downloads.

He is very interested in distributed computing and related programming. He has also developed a transparent distributed object system MetaVM, which is based on shuJIT, and MOBA, a Java thread migration software. Both are distributed technologies and freely downloadable.

He says he is enjoying life with his wife and working as a research scientist. His technical interests are on distributed computing, programming language and information security. He spends free time hacking software, whistling and driving.

Reported May 2004
